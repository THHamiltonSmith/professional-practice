Current employability

Part 1.1 pre-reflection on my current employability, professional goals, skill gaps, and readiness for employment or further study.

Through this employability process, I want to become better prepared and more confident as I move from university into the next stage of my career. I am in my final semester of Advanced Computer Science, so I have started thinking more seriously about how my degree, personal software projects, and professional development align with real workplace expectations. My main career goal is to work in software engineering, artificial intelligence, or machine learning research. Longer term, I would also like to build and run my own company, so I know I need to keep developing as both a technical person and a professional.

One of my main focuses this semester is becoming employable beyond my technical ability. Technical skills are important in computer science, but they are not the only part of being ready for work. I want to keep improving my communication, teamwork, leadership, and overall professional confidence. I feel that communication is one of my stronger areas, especially when explaining ideas or talking through projects, but I also know that professional communication is more than speaking clearly. It involves listening properly, understanding different perspectives, adapting to different teams, and presenting myself in a way that feels credible and respectful.

I also want to improve how I work with others. Much of my experience has come from self-directed learning and personal projects, where I have had to solve problems independently and make quick decisions. That has helped me build initiative and resilience, but industry work often depends on collaboration. I want to get better at handling feedback, managing expectations, contributing to shared goals, and understanding how decisions are made in teams. I think this will help me become someone who can contribute in both technical and non-technical situations.

Another goal is to gain greater clarity about my future pathway. At this stage, I feel ready to apply for graduate employment, but I am also considering postgraduate study, including master's or research opportunities in Australia or overseas. Because of that, I want to build a stronger understanding of what employers, universities, and research programs are looking for. This includes improving my professional self-presentation, becoming more aware of industry expectations, and learning how to communicate the value of my skills and experiences more clearly.

Overall, I believe I have a strong foundation built on my degree, independent projects, and an interest in technology and innovation. At the same time, I do not want to rely solely on technical ability. I want to become a more well-rounded graduate who can communicate effectively, work effectively with others, lead when needed, continue learning, and adapt to diverse professional environments. By engaging in employability activities this semester, I hope to gain greater confidence in my direction and be better prepared for employment, postgraduate study, and the broader world of work.

Personal Goal Setting Training with Sol Bushby

Reflection one
Situation
I attended the Personal Goal Setting Training session with Sol Bushby during Semester 1. The session focused on personal goals, discipline, routines, and how everyday habits can affect business and career outcomes. I connected with this because I have strong ambitions around software engineering, AI, and eventually building my own company, but those goals only matter if they are backed by consistency and discipline.
Task
My goal was to understand how to set goals that lead to action. I wanted to improve how I approach long-term goals, especially because I often have multiple projects or ideas at once. I needed to connect personal discipline with professional progress, rather than relying on motivation only when I feel inspired.
Action
During the session, I listened to Sol explain how he built the routines needed to improve, compete, and keep going through adversity as an athlete. I reflected on how that approach could apply to study, work, and career development, especially the difference between wanting an outcome and building the daily habits that make it realistic.
Result
The session made me recognise that professional success is often built from personal systems. It is not just about setting big goals, but having the discipline to work towards them consistently. I came away with a clearer understanding of how routines, resilience, and self-management shape professional outcomes.
Learning
The main lesson was that motivation is not enough by itself. To achieve long-term goals, I need habits that make progress consistent, even when I am not feeling motivated. This matters for employability because employers value people who can manage themselves, stay focused, and keep improving.
Personal / Professional change
This experience made me think more seriously about how I approach my goals. It pushed me to be more honest about where I need more discipline, especially when balancing university, work, and personal projects. Professionally, it reminded me that employability also depends on reliability, consistency, and working towards difficult goals over time.

Intro to B2B Sales Program

Reflection two
Situation
The Intro to B2B Sales Program was a multi-week workshop I completed through work at a startup. It focused on pitching a product, speaking with potential customers, and communicating value in a business-to-business environment. This was relevant because I am interested in software engineering and entrepreneurship, and it gave me the chance to practise skills that are not always taught directly in a computer science degree.
Task
My task was to improve my understanding of how businesses sell products and communicate with customers. As someone who often focuses on building software, I wanted to get better at explaining a product's value in a way customers understand. I also wanted to ask better questions, pitch more clearly, and understand what motivates businesses to buy.
Action
Throughout the program, I took part in sales training, listened to industry examples, and considered how the strategies could apply to my work and software projects. We learned about customer acquisition, interviews, pitching, scaling, and direct communication. A key lesson was to avoid overcomplicating the message and clearly explain the problem, solution, and action you want the customer to take.
Result
The program changed how I think about business communication. I realised that even a strong technical product can fail if its value is not communicated properly. I also learned that direct communication is often more effective than sounding overly detailed or technical, especially in startups where people need to understand value quickly.
Learning
The biggest lesson was that selling is not just about convincing people. It is about understanding the customer, identifying their problem, and showing how your product solves it. This matters because software engineers often need to communicate with clients, managers, users, and non-technical stakeholders.
Personal / Professional change
This program made me more confident connecting technical work to business value. It helped me think more like a professional rather than only a student or developer. I now better understand that employability in software also means explaining why something matters, who it helps, and how it creates value.

Tech eChallenge

Reflection three
Situation
The Tech eChallenge was one of my most important professional development experiences in Semester 1. I worked with a friend to develop an MVP for one of my personal projects and pitch it to a mock panel of investors. We made it through the semi-finals and into the final nine teams, eventually finishing fourth out of around 35 teams. This stood out because it connected directly to my goal of building real software products and potentially running my own company.
Task
Our task was to take an idea and develop it into something we could present professionally. We needed to explain the problem, define the solution, understand the market, and show why the product was worth supporting. For me, the challenge was not only building the product, but communicating it clearly under pressure to people judging it from a business perspective.
Action
I worked on shaping the product concept, preparing the pitch, practising delivery, and improving how we explained the MVP's value. We had to make the idea clear and persuasive, not just technically interesting. This involved refining the pitch, thinking about customer pain points, presenting the project professionally, and managing nerves in a more stressful environment than a normal university presentation.
Result
We progressed through the competition and finished fourth overall. This gave me evidence that I can present a product idea in a competitive environment. More importantly, it showed me that I can take a personal project and turn it into something that can be explained, judged, and taken seriously by others.
Learning
The main lesson was that a good product needs more than good technology. It needs a clear story, a strong pitch, and the ability to communicate value quickly. I also learned how much preparation matters because each round of practice made the pitch more confident and professional.
Personal / Professional change
This experience had a major impact on my confidence. It made me feel more capable of presenting my own ideas publicly and gave me a better understanding of what it takes to pitch a product professionally. It also changed how I see my personal projects: as professional opportunities that require business thinking, communication, confidence, and development skills.

What changed overall?

At the beginning of the semester, I felt I had a strong foundation from my computer science degree, work experience, and personal software projects. The biggest change is that I now understand employability is not just technical capability. Technical skill still matters, but communication, discipline, confidence, teamwork, and the ability to explain value are just as important in software engineering, startups, and future research or postgraduate pathways.

The activities helped me connect my ambitions to practical skills: the goal setting session reinforced discipline and consistency, the B2B sales program showed the value of direct communication, and the Tech eChallenge gave me real experience pitching under pressure. Overall, I feel more prepared to present myself, communicate ideas clearly, and connect my technical background to industry value. I am still interested in software engineering, AI/ML, and potentially further study, but whichever path I choose, I need to keep developing as a whole professional, not just as a technical student.
